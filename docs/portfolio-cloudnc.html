Website Development, Software UI Design and Development : CloudNC

Intro

CloudNC automate the end-to-end manufacturing process and utilise automatic toolpath generation techniques, enabling high volumes of parts to be produced and shipped worldwide, saving thousands of human hours in the process.

I joined CloudNC on a three-month contract in January 2018, and was tasked to rebuild their existing website, which was housed in a monorepo together with their Autonomous Machining Technology (AMT) software.

Towards the end of the contract, I received extremely positive feedback from the frontend team lead and was offered the opportunity to join their technology team permanently.

Process and Methodology

I worked in an agile technology team of six frontend developers and one UX designer, which was part of a wider "full stack" team of around 15 engineers in total.

I worked in sprints lasting two weeks, reporting to a Software Development Team Lead and Head of Product, and the team was managed by the VP of Engineering. We used JIRA to manage tasks end-to-end and held sprint planning meetings, backlog grooming sessions and retrospectives fortnightly, and stand-ups daily. The Fibonacci sequence was adopted to size tasks accordingly, which helped to accurately measure each sprint's progress against a burndown chart.

Key Projects

My role at CloudNC encompassed three major rebranding exercises:

1. Website Development

In fulfilling a business critical need and working to an externally imposed deadline, I worked in a team of two and built a responsive, optimised and semantic website to aid CloudNC in their Series A fundraising journey.

Within the context of Angular components, I wrote modularised HTML and Sass for CloudNC's customer-facing brand website (no longer publicly visible). Contained as part of a broader Angular application, this involved a learning curve of Angular component building and modification, Angular Material (Material Design component and theming library) and how to approach global and view encapsulated styles within the context of modular components. I ensured best coding practices were followed with due attention to detail.

2. AMT Software Design and Development

The AMT software is many things, but for all intents and purposes it is a multi-level, interactive and highly sophisticated CNC machining simulator, g-code generator and tooling library. Integrated with a complex backend of mathematical toolpath generation, it takes a 3D file and graphically displays a series of toolpaths in combination with the specialised cutting tools needed to manufacture a part. These toolpaths are derived as the fastest and most efficient from millions of possible permutations.

CNC Machinists use this software to generate a setup sheet (more information below) which in turn provides them key information such as the physical tools needed for a particular job, tool specifications and the estimate time per machining operation.

I was responsible for the UI design and its application in theming the software.

Setup Sheet

A setup sheet is a document which instructs the CNC Machinist what part they are producing, the tools needed and the estimated time the job will take to run. In ascertaining feedback from the machinisits I learnt that the initial setup sheet lacked key information, was poorly designed and its layout was confusing.

I worked closely with the UX designer to come up with new designs for both the digital and printed setup sheet templates. These underwent a few rounds of iterations before being implemented within the AMT software interface.

The redesigned setup sheet was very well received by Machinists: it served as a time saver on a per job basis, being easier to understand with key information presented in the most logical order.

3. Digital Production Dashboard

The factory based Operations team required a dashboard display of all current jobs (work centres), which continually updated in real time, as production jobs were queued and flowed through the factory floor. A production job could be: procurement, quality control or indeed a machine itself. Information was to be displayed on a television screen, used as a source of reference to monitor work centre statuses, ascertain machining status and capacity, plan for procurement of materials and separately, evaluate uptime and downtime of machinery.

I was responsible for project managing this piece of work end-to-end, ensuring internal deadlines were met and liaising with the relevant stakeholders. It was my duty to collaborate with the Operations Associate to ascertain and understand deeply all of the requirements of this project so that they could be prioritised.

UX Design

During my research I determined that there are 48 work centres in total. Given the information to be displayed per work centre, my perception was that it would prove too difficult to arrange 48 work centres on a single screen. Subsequently, it made sense to alternate periodically between two views (24 work centres per view). I maintained a thought that this seemed unachievable, although it was without doubt more viable.

This led me to create a wireframe divided into eight columns, whereby each column would contain a maximum of three work centres.

UI design

The first design ideas I came up with were colour coded to match the UI design of the AMT software. This was a desired requirement from the Operations team, and so it made sense to reproduce a similar style. Colours were indicative of machine status, and job/part information was displayed.

While the initial design was mostly well received, it wasn't quite workable in that the doughnut graph needed redesigning due to amended requirements.

As well as quantity made and quantity remain it needed to also show quantity total. After some ideas and sketches around how best to illustrate this data, I came up with the following design, whereby: quantity made is represented by the blue zone, quantity remaining orange, and quantity total shown as the white outline.

This design of a single work centre was widely appreciated and the Operations Associate and his team were happy to proceed. An example of the finalised design is shown below. This was extremely well received by the Operations team and serves as a useful aid in monitoring work centre activity, monitoring behaviours as well as enabling factory employees to carry out their job more effectively.
